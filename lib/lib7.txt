Iterative approach : 
You build the system in repeated iterations. 
Each cycle includes planning, design, implementation, 
and testing for a slice of the product. 
You get working versions early, collect feedback, 
and refine the next iteration. 
Requirements can evolve as you learn.

Waterfall method : 
You move through phases in a mostly one-way sequence: 
requirements to design to implementation to testing to 
deployment. You typically don’t go back unless something 
breaks badly. It requires thorough planning from the 
beginning and if something goes wrong, it is hard to adapt, 
thus less flexible. 

4. 
Everyone knows what “done” means per phase and who owns what. 
Easier to estimate timelines, costs, and resources; progress is measurable.
Problems get caught earlier (requirements and design reviews, testing plans), reducing expensive late rework.
Stakeholders know when they should give input (requirements, approvals, acceptance testing), so fewer surprises.
Standard checkpoints (reviews, testing gates) improve reliability and documentation.

5. TRUE
A big point of SDLC is identifying and addressing problems early 
(requirements and design validation, reviews, prototyping), 
so you don’t discover fundamental issues only after 
you’ve already coded a lot.
